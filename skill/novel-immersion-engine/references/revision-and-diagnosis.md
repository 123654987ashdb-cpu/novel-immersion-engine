# Revision And Diagnosis

Use this reference when the user provides existing outline, chapter, scene, or manuscript text and wants critique, rewrite, strengthening, or continuation.

## Diagnostic Order

Do not polish lines until the structure can carry the reader.

1. **Reader experience**: Where does curiosity rise or fall? Where does the reader feel bored, confused, unconvinced, emotionally distant, or rewarded?
2. **Hierarchy fit**: Does the chapter serve its stage, does the stage serve its volume, and does the volume still advance the selected novel master outline?
3. **Promise and payoff**: What did the story make the reader expect? What has it paid? What is unpaid but still alive?
4. **Promise debt and direction**: Which promises have stalled, which new hooks overload attention, and does current writing still serve author intent and current focus?
5. **Causality**: Does the previous event cause the next? Does each choice constrain later options?
6. **Character agency**: Does the character choose under pressure, or only get moved by plot?
7. **Character change**: Is change proven by costly behavior, not declared?
8. **Relationship movement**: Does trust, power, intimacy, debt, secrecy, or resentment shift?
9. **Core cost**: If the novel is built on loss or transformation, does each relevant chapter advance or deepen it perceptibly?
10. **Knowledge boundary**: Does anyone know, infer, or verbalize facts beyond what the story earned?
11. **Scene function**: Does each scene have desire, obstacle, tactic, turn, consequence?
12. **Continuity**: Are timeline, knowledge, injuries, objects, names, rules, power levels, and locations consistent?
13. **Key objects and resources**: Do meaningful items and limited quantities retain valid holder/state, witnessed history, payoff purpose, and consequence?
14. **Abilities and item effects**: Did a skill, power, technique, equipment effect, unlock, upgrade, charge, or counter stay within established source, boundary, cost, and setup?
15. **Subplots and cadence**: Have promised side lines vanished or recent chapters repeated a function, mood, reward, setting, or ending without transformed meaning?
16. **Voice continuity**: Does the prose preserve or deliberately alter narrator distance, attention pattern, rhythm, and dialogue identities?
17. **Pacing**: Are pressure and release deliberately placed? Are payoffs delayed too long?
18. **Line level**: Are paragraphs and sentences serving function, rhythm, POV, and natural prose?

## Immersion Failure Modes

- **No active reader question**: the reader does not know what they are waiting for.
- **No cost**: success or failure changes too little.
- **Static chapter**: plot, character, relationship, information, pressure, and promise are nearly unchanged.
- **Static scene**: entry and exit states match.
- **Decorative prose**: language is pretty but does not alter attention, pressure, character, world, or meaning.
- **Explained emotion**: the text names feelings before dramatizing behavior, perception, rhythm, or choice.
- **False cliffhanger**: the ending looks tense but resolves without consequence.
- **Delayed everything**: the chapter opens questions but pays none.
- **Exposition before need**: lore appears before it creates pressure or solves an active question.
- **Character moved by author**: decisions happen because plot needs them, not because desire, fear, and pressure converge.
- **Sudden growth**: a character changes without repeated tests, cracks, cost, and proof.
- **Unfair reveal**: the truth was hidden by omission, unnatural silence, or lack of plant.
- **Cost drift**: a premise-defining loss or transformation stops being tracked, is repeated without escalation, or suddenly jumps without evidence.
- **Knowledge leak**: a character acts, infers, or speaks with author-level access to information they cannot possess.
- **Theme spoken for the reader**: a character explains the novel's intended meaning instead of the story making it felt.
- **Object discontinuity**: an important item appears, disappears, transfers, breaks, or gains meaning without traceable cause and witness.
- **Voice drift**: a new chapter no longer sounds like the established narrator or speaking characters without a deliberate story reason.
- **Draft accident canonized**: an unplanned detail introduced during generation is silently treated as approved story fact.
- **Promise debt overload**: new hooks keep opening while earlier reader commitments stop progressing.
- **Cadence loop**: chapters repeat the same pressure, reward, location shape, or ending image without escalation or changed meaning.
- **Resource reset**: limited money, medicine, countdown, access, measurable cost, or other resource changes without traceable settlement.
- **Subplot disappearance**: a side line that promised consequence vanishes without intentional dormancy or resolution.
- **Authority overwrite**: a local request or selected future beat silently contradicts accepted prose or the author's non-negotiable direction.
- **Canon drift**: the story forgets who knows what, where objects are, or what rules limit action.

## Strengthening Moves

Use the smallest move that fixes the root cause.

- Enter later, closer to disturbance.
- Give the protagonist a visible want in the scene.
- Add an obstacle that forces tactic change.
- Make the easy plan fail.
- Convert delay into cost.
- Put two goals in conflict.
- Force a public or irreversible choice.
- Pay one old promise while opening a new one.
- Advance a stale promise before introducing another promise family.
- Change chapter cadence by switching reward, scene container, relationship function, or ending shape while preserving arc pressure.
- Move a reveal earlier if boredom is caused by withheld clarity.
- Move a reveal later if suspense needs fair suspicion first.
- Replace explanation with behavior, object, silence, or consequence.
- Reuse an existing setup instead of inventing a new fact.
- Protect the strongest emotional scene and repair logic around it when possible.

## Webnovel Retention Check

For serialized fiction, every chapter should support three layers:

```markdown
## Retention Layers
- Immediate hook:
- Mini-arc payoff in 3-10 chapters:
- Long-arc promise:
- Visible progress marker:
- Reader reward delivered:
- New question:
- Promise debt advanced/resolved rather than merely mentioned:
- Cadence variation from recent chapters:
- Core cost progression, if the novel is driven by loss:
- Witness record or absence, if central to the hook:
```

Reader rewards include:

- Grievance -> pressure -> reversal -> recognition.
- Limit -> risk/training -> breakthrough -> stronger problem.
- Friction -> care -> intimacy -> new emotional obstacle.
- Clue -> misread -> evidence -> deeper question.
- Move -> counter-move -> hidden cost -> status shift.
- Wound -> connection -> trust -> relapse or deeper healing.

Do not force every serial novel into conventional victory-based 爽点. First identify its true retention engine. It may be triumph, intimacy, mystery, dread, moral decline, or the painful question of what remains of a character after each cost.

## Line Revision

Line edit only after the chapter works.

Check:

- Does this paragraph have one primary job?
- Does the first sentence orient or pull?
- Does the last sentence push, land, or turn?
- Does the POV character genuinely notice this?
- Would this character use this metaphor or judgment?
- Is emotion dramatized before it is named?
- Are repeated reactions compressed?
- Are transitions earned by cause and effect rather than template connectors?
- Does the Chinese prose avoid machine-like symmetry, abstract padding, and repeated grand closing sentences?

Line actions:

- Keep.
- Cut.
- Merge.
- Move.
- Dramatize.
- Replace abstract claim with concrete action.
- Replace generic description with POV-specific perception.
- Break rhythm for shock, breath, hesitation, or impact.
- Restore quietness where over-polish damages mood.

## Continuation Checklist

Before continuing an existing novel, extract:

- Its inferred or confirmed position in `小说总纲 -> 分卷纲 -> 卷内阶段纲 -> 章节纲`.
- Latest confirmed canon.
- Current plot state.
- Current character states.
- Current relationship states.
- Open setups and unpaid promises.
- Active reader questions.
- What the protagonist knows.
- What opponents know.
- What must not be contradicted.
- Key object holders, conditions, and meanings.
- Author intent, current focus, and any active override.
- Promise debt board and promises due soon.
- Recent cadence trail.
- Countable resource and subplot states, if active.
- Current narrator/character voice fingerprints.
- Next chapter's required inheritance.

For large manuscripts, process chapter batches and consolidate an authoritative current-story state before designing future prose. Then continue by producing the next fixed-format chapter writing blueprint for the external prose model.
